The Story

While the rest of the world was swapping small block Chevys into everything with fenders, one machinist spent twenty years doing something nobody else would.

He started with a one-piece steel 1937 Plymouth Business Coupe — pre-war lines, suicide doors, a shape that stopped being made when the world had other things to worry about. And instead of dropping in the motor everyone expected, he reached for the other Hemi. The one Chrysler built before the 426 made the name famous.

A 1952 DeSoto FireDome 276 — the first year DeSoto ever put a Hemi in a car. He fitted it with MSD fuel injection and an RV cam, backed it with a TorqueFlite 727 and an 8" Mercury rear end. He gave it RideTech air ride, Wilwood brakes, and power everything. Not because it was easy. Because he’s a machinist, and a machinist doesn’t cut corners.

This isn’t a car you build from a catalog. This is what happens when a man with forty years of precision toolmaking decides to build exactly one car, and takes twenty years to get it right.
